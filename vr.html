What is Virtual Reality?

Virtual reality refers to the use of computer technology to create a simulated environment. Scenes and objects appear to be real, which makes the user feel immersed in their surroundings and essentially places the user inside the experience. If the user turns their head, the artificially created world turns with them so that the illusion is never lost. VR also simulates senses such as vision, hearing, touch, and even smell.








Technical Aspects of Virtual Reality - Where Are We Today?

Field of View

Currently, one of the biggest obstacles in creating a 100% immersive experience is the fact that humans have a much wider field of view than what can be provided with most of today’s headsets. However, many VR hardware designers are working on creating headsets that can provide a 180-degree field of view, which would be ideal for a highly immersive simulation.

Frame Rate

In order for VR headsets to track the user’s movement quickly and consistently, a high frame rate is necessary to maximize the experience. Many developers have found that frame rates less than 60 FPS may cause disorientation, headaches, and nausea, so most VR content is around 90 FPS. Going forward, many developers are trying to push for a frame rate of 120 FPS or more in order to provide a more true-to-life experience.

Sound Effects

Currently, cutting-edge VR often uses spatial audio, a technique that can create audio effects that match the visuals. In real life, the sounds that we hear can vary based on our location or how our head is turned. Spatial audio mimics this sensation using a variety of implementations, such as volume control, left/right channel delay to convey direction, head tracking to map auditory space, and manipulation of reverb and echo.

Head & Position Tracking

One of the most important aspects of VR is the fact that the virtual space can adjust to the user’s position. Currently, there are two types of head & position tracking applications: 3 degrees of freedom (3DoF) and 6 degrees of freedom (6DoF). Headsets that use 3DoF are only capable of rotational tracking, meaning they won’t pick up when the user moves their whole body. On the other hand, headsets that use 6DoF can track the user’s position and allow for full movement through the 3D space.


Examples of Applications of Virtual Reality:

Healthcare

Entertainment

Healthcare specialists can use virtual models to practice performing precise operations without needing to work on real bodies. VR can also be used for mental health issues, and has been proven to be effective in the treatment of PTSD and anxiety.

There is a strong presence of VR in the entertainment industry. It can be used for gaming (e.g Oculus Rift), and VR is also being introduced to cinemas and theme parks to make experiences more immersive.

Space & Military

Automotive Industry

These two industries operate in dangerous environments that can’t be easily accessed, so VR provides a way for trainees to prepare in conditions that are not dangerous but are as close to reality as possible.

VR can be used to help car manufacturers analyze road scenarios and the behaviour of cars. Simulated scenarios allow them to analyze and make changes to prototypes before releasing new models.
